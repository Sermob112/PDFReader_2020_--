OLHARDING is currently operating from three
sites in the northern part of The Netherlands.
Waterhuizen provides head-office and design facilities,
and Foxhol fabricates modules and accommodation
blocks for the main assembly facility at Eemshaven,
named as the building yard of B G Ireland. Here, these
are added to hulls constructed by third-parties, notably
by Daewoo Mangalia, in Romania, in line with an
arrangement which has already collectively produced
some 30 vessels.
B G Ireland
is of
single-deck,
double-skin
construction with three cargo holds. Access to No 1 is
through an extended forecastle which has the fore-part
covered by a whaleback structure affording cover to the
mooring area, and joined at its aft end with a breakwater
erected to provide protection to deck-stowed
containers. Each hold is fitted with cell guides, which, in
No 2 hold, can be moved to suit 40ft and 45ft
containers as required.
Carriage of 20ft/30ft/40ft/45ft, and 49ft units is
possible, and 100 reefer plugs are fitted. The hatch
covers are of the hydraulic folding type designed, along
with the adjacent deck, for maximum permissable stack
loads of 60/70tonnes for 20ft units, 80tonnes for 30ft
units, and 90tonnes for other sizes. In the holds, the
loadings are 70/105tonnes for TEU/FEU. The side and
bottom tanks in the double-hull space are separate, and
used for fuel or water ballast. A centreline duct keel
divides the bottom tanks, and fore and aft passageways
are formed at the top of the side tanks, port and
starboard.
The five-tier superstructure is built on the poop deck
and has the wheelhouse on top. In common with many
similar designs, it is positioned right aft above the single
CP propeller and rudder, with the engineroom forward
of this layout. The upper deck above this space is
available for stowage of two FEU stacks.
The hull is constructed with both longitudinal and
transverse framing, with extra stiffening added in way of
the forecastle to counter the effects of the vessel's high
speed. Additional stiffening is also introduced in the
double bottom area and this allows a tank top loading
of 6.37tonnes/m2 to be applied. All the bulkheads are
of flush construction and the transverse bulkheads run
from shell to shell.

ONSTRUCTION of this vessel is marked by a
number of 'firsts'. She is the first delivery from the
recently formed Kvaerner Philadelphia shipyard,
located within the Philadelphia Naval Base, and the
first new ship to be built at the base since the US Navy
Amphibious Command Vessel Blue Ridge, 34 years ago.
Manukai and her sister Maunawili are also the first
additions to the Matson fleet since 1992, when RJ
Pfeiffer entered service from the NASSCO shipyard
intended for operations on their owner's long-
established Los Angeles/Oakland/Seattle to Honolulu
route.
Re-development of the Philadelphia shipyard has
drawn heavily on assistance from Kvaerner's European
shipbuilding subsidiaries, particularly the Warnow yard,
and the design of this pioneer vessel, which is
designated the Philadelphia CV2600 container ship, is
based on that German shipyard's own CV2600 series.
Particularly noteworthy in this modified Philadelphia
version is the inclusion of 'green ship' features such as
grey and bilge water holding tanks, green water tanks,
and the use of tin-free coatings. No asbestos products
or PCBs are included, and the engines are arranged for
reduced noxious gas emissions.
Building Manukai has served as a valuable training
exercise for the new company as a prelude to entering
the commercial market place, and has been undertaken
in accordance with US Government Jones Act
requirements, as part of
a Title XI financing
programme. The double-hull layout follows the now
conventional container ship configuration, with a single
deck, forecastle, and sunken aft mooring deck. Eleven
hatches fitted with lift-on/lift-off pontoon covers serve
the six cargo holds, which are arranged forward and aft
of
a three-quarter-aft,
eight-tier superstructure
providing accommodation for 21 crew,
three
supernumaries and four-man riding crew.
The forward hold is accessed through an extended
forecastle, and is protected by a breakwater. In common
with the other cargo spaces, it is fitted with cell guides
suitable for 20ft/40ft containers. Stowage is also
arranged on the hatch covers, and over the mooring
deck, for units ranging in size from 20ft to 53ft. Reefer
plugs are provided for 260 containers on deck and 40 in
the hold, served by a Seacos monitoring system.
As the series designation would suggest, TEU
capacity for this new KPSI product was intended to be
around 2600, however after this notation had been
agreed, the addition of an extra deckhouse tier allowed
higher container stacks to be loaded, increasing capacity
to 2900TEU, then, by lengthening No 4 hold and
modifying the aft deck to carry larger containers, total
TEU capacity became 3000.
Manukai is powered by a Hyundai-built MAN B&W
main engine, developing a maximum continuous rating
of 28,800kW at 104rev/min, and driving a FP propeller
when running at 90% full power and a sea margin of20%. Electrical power is derived from four MaK diesel-
driven alternator sets, two of which produce 1360kW
and two 1530kW. Steam generation is by means of a
composite boiler producing 3200kg/h from each side.
Manoeuvring is assisted by fitting a Brunvoll 1300kW
bow thruster.
