RCTIC VOYAGER follows the design of the
Kawasaki-built LNG tanker Energy Advance
(Significant Ships of 2005) very closely, but with the
important addition of features enabling her to operate
successfully in the harsh Arctic environment
surrounding the emerging Norwegian Snøhvit LNG
production project. Despite its similarity to the earlier
ship, the design of these new vessels has been prepared
by Mitsui Engineering & Shipbuilding, and that
company has also delivered the first vessel to enter
service, drawing heavily on the accumulated experience
of Det Norske Veritas relating to the structural and
human problems of operating in Arctic conditions.
Arctic Voyager is the 16th Kawasaki-built LNG tanker
to use the Moss spherical cargo tank containment
system, and is fitted with four independent tanks of
identical size, holding a total of 142,700m3 at a filling
ratio of 98.5% and temperature of -163
oC. Like the
ship's main hull structure, the tanks, and tank skirt which
supports them,
satisfy the 25-year fatigue life
requirements of DNV Notations Plus-2 and CSA-2,
based on the dynamic loading of a North Atlantic wave
environment.
Special consideration has been given to items such as
anti-icing, de-icing, lifesaving equipment, working areas,
navigation systems, deck machinery and living quarters,
with regard to the cold operating conditions. In addition,
DNV COMF-V(1) requirements for vibration and noise
levels in the accommodation are complied with, along
with COMF-C(2), relating to air conditioning at very low
ambient temperatures. Extended, covered bridge wings
are another concession to the problems of harsh
weather.
The vessel is powered by a Kawasaki UA-400 turbine,
supplied with steam from two dual-fuel (HFO/boil-off
gas) watertube boilers and developing 27,000kW. A
double-reduction, tandem-articulated, reduction gearbox
is fitted in the drive to an FP propeller. Two steam
turbine-driven 3250kW alternators and a diesel-powered
set of similar output are installed to satisfy electric
loadings. Double-hull fuel tanks and a high-performance
oily-water separator are amongst environmental features
adopted. Cargo handling is by means of eight (two for
each tank) electric submerged pumps, each of 1350m3/h
duty. Loading is accomplished by shore pumps.
A wave monitoring system is fitted, and sensors are
positioned at major hull structure parts to measure stress
levels caused by waves during navigation, whilst
manoeuvrability is enhanced by a high-lift Schilling
rudder and a bow thruster. Also installed is an
automated ballast exchange system.

ERGE NANTONG
is a fully refrigerated
LPG/NH3 tanker built to what has developed into
a somewhat standard layout for this type of vessel,
featuring a single-skin, flush decked hull without
forecastle, but with the upper deck given a slight sheer
forward, in lieu. The mooring deck aft is sunken and
the flat profile is relieved only by the five-tier
superstructure aft, housing the accommodation.
The cargo space, stretching forward from the
deckhouse, is divided into four holds each having top
and bottom wing ballast tanks, and containing an
independent, free-standing, saddle-supported prismatic
cargo tank manufactured from low-temperature carbon
steel. Designed for carrying cargoes at a minimum of
-50ºC and a maximum vapour pressure of 0.25bar, the
tanks are insulated with 120mm-thick polyurethane
foam clad with 0.5mm aluminium/zinc steel sheet.
Cargoes of type 2G can be loaded, including
anhydrous ammonia, 1,3-butadiene, butane (both ISO
and normal), butylenes, commercial propane, and
propylene, together with other products whose toxicity,
aggressiveness, pressure, temperature, and specific
gravity are within the limits of the design. Cargo is
handled by means of
two Hamworthy-Svanehøj
deepwell pumps in each tank (eight in total), each
having a capacity of 600m3/h and capable of providing
a discharge time of approximately 18hours, excluding
stripping. Loading is also accomplished in about
18hours, based on vapour return to shore.
The engineering affiliate of
Hyundai Heavy
Industries built the MAN B&W 6S60MC-C main
engine installed in Berge Nantong.
This develops
13,560kW MCR at 105rev/min, enabling the vessel to
sail at a service speed of 16.75knots at an NCR of
12,044kW (89% MCR). The same company also
manufactured under licence the three MAN B&W
5L28/32H prime movers for the alternator sets
(produced by Hyundai's electrical division), which each
supply 1000kW to satisfy electrical requirements.

HE significance of this new, five-ship class of
Norwegian coastal ferry lies not so much with the
vessel design, but with the choice of propelling
machinery and its operation. Built to provide the
necessary waterborne links on the major E39 road
between Bergen and Stavanger, where they will replace
existing vessels operating between Halhjem/Sandvikvåg,
and Aarsvågen/Mortavika, the basic design follows that
of the many double-ended ferries working on the
Norwegian coast. Thus, they have a hull given over to
vehicle accommodation and accessed from either end,
surmounted by a central superstructure topped with a
navigating bridge, from which the vessel can be conned
in the required sailing direction, thus obviating time-
consuming turning manoeuvres.
Two versions of this LMG Marin design are being built.
In each case the hulls, which are of the same dimensions,
are being constructed at the Aker Tulcea shipyard in
Romania, following which they are towed to the Aker
Brattvåg (Søviknes) site in Norway for fitting out.
Bergensfjord is the first unit of a larger vehicle capacity, and
slightly faster, three-ship group carrying 212 private
cars/22 trailers on two decks, with the other two vessels
loading 145 cars on a single deck. Vehicle access is by way
of doors/ramps and visors at each end, and passengers
enter through side doors in the superstructure, within
which lounge-type seating is provided for 587 persons.
Seven double- and four single-berth cabins are available
for crew.
The innovative machinery installation adopted for both
alternatives of this new design is centred upon the use of
liquid natural gas as the main fuel for the propulsion plant.
Clean LNG is readily available from Norway's North Sea
oil fields, and a coastal supply and distribution network for
bunkering is currently being established, since this fuel's
use as a replacement for marine diesel forms a major part
of the government's plans for restriction of 'greenhouse'
gas emissions. This particularly follows claims that 40%
of the country's NOx discharges come from its fishing
boat, ferry and other coastal shipping sources.
The Bergen Diesel division of Rolls-Royce has
accumulated experience of building land-based, gas-
powered engines dating back over many years, and has
two proven designs currently available: the B35:40V
developing 440kW/cylinder, and the smaller K-G4 model
of 222kW cylinder output. This latter engine is the type
selected for the Bergensfjord series, and is the first marine
version to be produced.
The design follows that of land-based machines by
adopting the Otto-cycle lean-burn combustion system,
claimed to have advantages over dual-fuel proposals
offered by some other manufacturers, which are
understood to have problems such as mis-firing and
difficult operation at low loads. In addition, the Rolls-
Royce design is said to have speedy acceleration and a fast-
stop ability which will be advantageous when approaching
terminals at relatively high speed.
Bergensfjord's machinery installation comprises a gas-
electric system based on two A van Kaick main
alternators, the larger of which develops 3530kW and is
driven by a Rolls-Royce KVGS-16G4 engine, with the
second, smaller, 2650kW set connected to a KVGS-12G4
prime mover. Power generated from these machines is
directed to the drive motors of the four azimuthing
propulsion units supplied by Schottel, a long-established
expert in this field.
These propulsors are positioned at each 'corner' of the
hull, and are of the recently introduced Schottel Combi
Drive (SCD) design. This combines the attributes of the
newer pod drives with those of the earlier, highly
successful mechanical Rudderpropeller models (but with
the electric drive motor fitted vertically in the support
tube of the unit instead of within the pod).
Bergensfjord and its two sisters are each fitted with SCD
2020, twin-propeller versions of the Schottel units, with a
propeller at each end of the pod and a capability for
azimuthing through 360deg. Each has a rating of 2750kW,
which allows these three vessels to operate the Halhjem-
to-Sandvikvåg service around the clock at 21knots. The
two smaller vessels in the series employ less powerful
1600kW Schottel propulsion units of the different
STP1515 type to give a slower, 17knot performance on
the Aarsvågen-Mortavika route.

ORWEGIAN naval architectural consultancy
Skipskonsulent, now operating as part of the
equally well-known Vik-Sandvik consultancy group, is
currently expanding its tanker-design portfolio, and
some examples of the varying range of ships of this
type featured there can be found elsewhere in this
review. The company is much involved with the
Chinese shipbuilding industry, and at the present time
has vessels under construction to its designs in the
Qingshan and Jinling yards, as well as with Shanghai
Edward, builder of Bit Okland and a sister to be
completed early next year.
These vessels are examples of Skipskonsulent's
SK4326, 25,000dwt IMO Type II chemical/product
tankers, and as such they exhibit their designer's
emphasis on safety by featuring a twin-skeg hull with
redundancy in the form of twin medium-speed engines,
two CP propellers, and twin rotary-vane steering gears
controlling two rudders which, together with a powerful
bow thruster of
1450kW output,
express the
manoeuvring abilities of the design, and contribute to a
facility for making quick turn-arounds in port - a feature
further enhanced by the inclusion of a 'super stripping'
cargo handling system for emptying tanks quickly.
Bit Okland is a double-hull tanker with eight pairs of
cargo tanks of varying sizes to suit parcel cargoes. A
poop and forecastle are provided, linked by a trunk on
the upper deck, which is positioned on the centreline.
The tank divisions are formed by a corrugated
centreline and transverse bulkheads, and, with deck
stiffening external, and side framing within the double
hull space, a plain surface is provided suitable for a
phenolic epoxy coating. Two slop tanks are built into
the fore end of the poop, and an interesting shelter is
arranged on the forecastle protecting the mooring
position. Eight cargo segregations can be handled using
16 submerged pumps of 375m3/h duty, capable of a
discharge duty of 3000m3/h with only half the pumps
running.
The machinery installation is centred upon two
Wärtsilä 6L46-B main engines, burning heavy fuel oil,
each developing 5850kW MCR at 514rev/min, and
coupled to its own CP propeller by way of a reduction
gearbox. Two 1875kVA alternators from A van Kaick
are driven from power take-offs on each gearbox, and
additional electric supply is derived from two alternators
connected to Wärtsilä 1360kW diesel prime-movers.
Two Gesab oil-fired thermal-oil heaters are fitted, each
having an output of 3800kW.

Canadian-based GTR Campbell consultancy
introduced its first standard cargo vessel - the celebrated
Freedom Liberty-ship replacement, to the international
market. Since then, Campbell, and its Bahamian associate
Algoship, has regularly had on offer a successful portfolio
of series-produced, mainly Handysize bulk carrier
designs. Some have been presented in earlier editions of
Significant Ships.
Latest of these is the Trader type, a
29,300dwt double-skin, five-hold vessel, developed in
conjunction with trading partners Clipper Group,
handling chartering and operations, and Dockendale
Shipping, looking after technical management.
As with most recent Campbell/Algoship designs, the
Trader class is being built in association with an emerging
or re-emerging Asian shipyard: in this case, the Cochin
Shipyard, with other contracts placed elsewhere in India
and China, now totalling more than 20 newbuildings.
Bossclip Trader is currently operating in the parcel bulk
trades between Brazil and the US Gulf, in a partnership
between Clipper and Boss, of Rio de Janeiro.
She conforms with a typical bulk carrier layout having
1.4m wide side tanks joined within a 1.5m deep double
bottom to provide combined side and bottom wing tanks
and a central bottom tank. The two longitudinal
bulkheads, which form the interior of the cargo space, are
widely spaced in accordance with Algoship fashion,
allowing hatches to occupy some 75% of the beam and to
present minimum overhangs.
Hatch covers are hydraulically-operated, end-folding
pairs, designed by Seohae and fabricated by the
shipbuilder. Other features of the cargo space are small
side hoppers, square bulkhead stools, and troughed
transverse bulkheads between the holds. Cargo is handled
by four sets of Tsuji HDSS 3026 hydraulically operated,
level-luffing deck cranes, of 30tonne x 26m radius and
mounted on tall pedestals to facilitate stowage of deck
cargoes. The cranes are positioned between holds number
1 and 2:2/3:3/4 and 4/5, with a capability for operation
with radio-controlled grabs.
The main engine is a MAN B&W 6S42MC Mk7 unit
manufactured by STX, in Korea, and developing 6480kW
MCR when running at 136rev/min to give a service speed
of 14.0knots. Electrical supply is delivered from three
Yanmar/Taiyo 500kW diesel-alternator sets, and a
composite boiler produces 1500kg/h/1100kg/h of
steam. Complement comprises eight officers and 13 crew
together with three spare cabins and six repair crew,
served by a Beihai 30-person, free-fall lifeboat operating
over the stern.

RGUABLY one of Sweden's oldest shipping
companies, the Broström group can trace its
origins back to Axel Broström, who commenced
operations as long ago as 1865. The fleet of almost 40
tankers now owned by the company is of varied size,
and includes medium and large-sized vessels engaged
in worldwide trading, and smaller designs involved in
European distribution activities. The latter is the area
of
employment for which Skipskonsulent has
developed this new SK4228AP series of
four,
14,700dwt chemical/product tankers, which Broström
has designated its D-class. The Norwegian consultancy
has consolidated a strong relationship with Chinese
builders over recent years, and these newbuildings
extend that association to the Jinling yard.
Redundancy and environmental considerations have
been prime considerations in formulating the design,
with an unusual choice of propulsion reflecting the
owner's requirements for good reliability and safety,
features which qualify the vessel for the award of the
additional Det Norske Veritas RP notation. The
machinery installation is centred on the use of twin
Rolls-Royce Azipull azimuthing thrusters as the main
propulsors.
Fitted into port and starboard pods in a stern
arrangement including a central skeg, each is driven by
a MAN B&W Alpha 7L27/38 medium-speed engine
developing 2380kW, to give the ship a service speed of
13knots. The mechanical propulsors, which incorporate
pulling propellers, afford an independent steering
capability as well as redundancy,
gained from
independent fuel systems. The ability to obtain precise
ship handling by directing propulsive thrust over a full
360deg, coupled with the availability of a 1000kW
tunnel-type bow thruster, should greatly facilitate
manoeuvrability and allow ships of the SK4228AP
design to dispense with tugs in most situations.
These Broström D-class vessels are believed to be the
first tankers to receive DNV's Clean Design notation,
based on their environmental qualities, which include
the fitting of
Munters SCR (selective catalytic
reduction) units to the main and auxiliary engines; these
are understood to cut NOx emissions by as much as
98%. Each system also includes an oxidation catalyst
stage, designed to ensure that carbon monoxide
emissions do not increase over the converter, whilst an
integrated silencer section has also been introduced to
keep noise levels low, in line with Broström's own,
exacting criteria. Electrical requirements are served
from four alternators fitted with MAN B&W 6L16/24
diesel engines built in China under licence.
Bro Deliverer is a single-decked vessel with poop,
forecastle and double-skin hull, conforming to Ice 1C
rules. A centreline bulkhead and troughed transverse
bulkheads divide the cargo space into 13 cargo tanks of
different sizes, each served by a Framo stainless steel,
submerged pump of 385m3/h capacity. With deck
stiffening arranged externally and side framing within
the double-hull space, the internal surfaces of the tanks
are plain and coated with Tankguard phenolic-epoxy.

IGNIFICANT SHIPS OF 2003 featured a vessel
(Amaranta) described, when delivered, as 'Poland's
largest box ship', and Charlotte Wulff, currently sailing as

Maersk, was originally intended to conform to that
same 8234 design but was modified before building
began to include the owner's/charterer's requirements
for increased reefer capacity and improved fuel
economy. A particular expertise of Gdynia naval
architects has been to refine - to very good effect - base
container ship concepts to meet particular owners'
needs.
These alterations have now been included by the
Gdynia shipyard into a new 8276-type container ship,
which retains the seven holds forward/one aft layout of
the earlier design but with the superstructure moved
further aft. This arrangement thereby allows one FEU
container stack to be transferred from the after deck
above the small No 8 hold, to the foredeck above the
machinery room.
A feature of the general arrangement of both the
8234 and the 8276 designs is the rectangular shape of
the upper deck, which allows a full 13 container rows to
be loaded almost to the forecastle, and to stow from a
maximum seven tiers high on No 7B hatch to three at
the bow. In addition, eight tiers can now be carried in
the new position above the engineroom where lashing
bridges are fitted.
All the deck stacks can stow TEU and FEU, except
right aft where only FEU are carried. Arrangements are
also included to allow 45ft boxes to be loaded in
selected positions on deck. In the forward holds, 11
rows (above the second tier) can be stowed in seven
tiers of 8ft 6in-high boxes, topped with one of 9ft 6in,
using a 40ft fixed cell-guide structure, reinforced at the
bottom for 20ft paired containers.
Compared with 450 total on Amaranta, electrical
connections for 400 reefer FEU are provided on deck,
and 300 in hold Nos 2 to 7, with a 4500m3/h air supply
for all reefer boxes supplied to these holds when each is
carrying a full load of between 30 and 60 boxes.
Dangerous cargoes can be loaded in holds 1, 2, 3 and 4
and on deck, except in way of the machinery space.
Although Amaranta was fitted with a Sulzer main
engine, other ships of that 8234 design are powered by
a MAN B&W 8K98MC-C unit; however, this has been
changed for Charlotte Wulff, which has a seven-cylinder,
Mk 6 version of the K98MC-series engine. By reducing
the number of cylinders, a substantial reduction in fuel
consumption has been secured, whilst replacing the
MC-C model with an MC type running at 94rev/min
instead of 104rev/min, is expected to improve the
propeller cavitation performance. The 7K98MC engine
develops 40,040kW at 94rev/min and produces a fast
service speed of 24.5knots at 90% MCR, only
marginally less than Amaranta.
To ensure that reducing the main engine size did not
unduly affect service speed, extensive model tests were
carried out at the CTO tank to improve the body lines
of the new design. These have resulted in the fitting of
a stern extension of approximately 5m to incorporate a
duck tail, aimed at improving resistance and seakeeping.
Because of the increase in reefer-box intake, the
electrical loading of the 8276 design has been increased,
and five alternators have been installed to meet the
demand. All are of MAN B&W/Dozamel manufacture,
with three having an output of 1890kW, and two
1680kW.

HE number of times the headline 'the world's
largest container ship' has been used to introduce
an entry in the 16 annual reviews which, so far, make
up the Significant Ships series, is anyone's guess. In our
first issue (1990) it might have been applied to either of
the two circa 2000TEU vessels featured, whilst last year
we referred to a vessel with a capacity four and a half
times larger: the 9200TEU MSC Pamela.
That report referred to even bigger things to come,
and now they have, with Möller-Maersk coyly admitting
to 11,000TEU slots, 1000 of which can be refrigerated,
available on this latest class. Many 'experts' suggest that
an even higher total would be nearer the mark, but does
it really matter what the true figure is, when ships
capable of carrying 13,000TEU are already in the
pipeline?  Necessarily, this report on the current 'world's
largest container ship' will be somewhat truncated since
Möller-Maersk, understandably, perhaps, in a highly
competitive world, does not readily divulge technical
details concerning its ships. However, despite the
absence of more information, Emma Maersk represents
a milestone in container ship design considered
sufficiently interesting as to warrant inclusion in this
2006 newbuilding review.
Basic dimensions, as recorded here, suggest that the
owner's design staff have adopted the usual method for
creating more box space, by increasing principal
dimensions, but there are limits here, enforced largely
by port restrictions, particularly on draught. Thus, with
beam one of the few variables, Emma Maersk emerges
with not too large an increase in length over previous
giants, but a massive 56m breadth moulded.
It has been said that 'when you have seen one giant
box-ship you have seen them all', but that is not quite
correct here. A sweeping bulwark links the forecastle to
the upper deck, improving a profile, made arguably
already more attractive than that of the average
container ship, by means of a distinctive balance. This
results from a tall deckhouse and funnel casing,
unusually positioned, compared with contemporaries,
almost at midships. The narrowness of this structure,
topped by a full-width, enclosed navigating bridge, is
unusual too, and necessitates special arrangements for
positioning the two 38-person totally enclosed lifeboats
and rescue craft.
Big ships need big engines, and this Maersk Line PS-
class vessel marks the introduction into service of the
first 14-cylinder in-line diesel engine. Wärtsilä Sulzer
takes the honours here (with MAN B&W developing
similar units, but none yet contracted), by supplying
14RT-flex96C engines developing 80,080kW to this
series. Four ABB TPL85-B turbochargers are fitted to
the engine. Other features of the machinery installation
worthy of note include the fitting of a 9.6m diameter
Mecklenburger 
Metallguss 
propeller 
weighing
135tonnes and turning at 102rev/min to drive the ship
at 25knots. This will enable the operator to maintain a
63-day round-trip voyage linking Europe and Asia, via
the Suez Canal.
Electrical requirements are served by five diesel-
driven alternators of 20,700kW combined output but a
notable addition is a waste heat recovery system uniting
a steam turbine with an ABB power turbine PTL3200
generator unit. This is installed to optimise onboard
energy production and delivers an extra maximum
output of 8500kW, allowing a fuel saving of more than
10%.
Two thrusters, each developing 25tonnes transverse
thrust, are fitted at each end of the vessel to assist with
manoeuvring, whilst other notable inclusions are
systems for flushing ballast water tanks at sea, and for

ERHAPS one of
the most significant ships
delivered from Turkey this year is FS Charlotte, an
11,000dwt combined molten sulphur and bitumen
tanker. Designed by Delta Marine, of Istanbul, this
interesting vessel was handed over to her owner,
Fouquet Sacop Maritime, of France, in March 2006, by
the Yardimci Shipyard group. She is one of only very
few vessels of this type in service worldwide and is now
on long-term charter to Total.
FS Charlotte is a single-decked, single-screw tanker,
with a double bottom and double skin, and has a total
of 11 cargo tanks and two slop tanks. Six of the cargo
tanks are designed to carry molten sulphur
(1.80tonne/m3) at 180ºC and five to carry bitumen
(1.30tonne/m3) at 250ºC. The cargo tanks are fully
independent of the hull structure, and mounted on
special supports, to allow for expansion of the cargo at
high temperatures; thus, FS Charlotte is, effectively, a
triple-hull ship.
Each of the cargo tanks, which can expand up to
80mm, weighs around 400tonnes, and this presented a
significant challenge to the designer. The Ulepsi tank
support system from the Dutch company Beele
Engineering was selected for the vessel; this system was
able to demonstrate that it could meet very specific
requirements, after extensive strength, thermal and
CFD analyses were carried out by Mesh Engineering
Co.
The cargo discharge system is based around four
Bornemann hydraulic pumps in two separate rooms.
Two pumps each have a capacity of 400m3/h, for
bitumen, and two are rated at 337m3/h for molten
sulphur.
Considerable attention also had to be paid to the
effectiveness of the tank insulation system, so that in
the event of any technical problems, the cargo
temperature can be maintained sufficiently to keep the
cargoes liquid, so that they can still be pumped ashore.
The daily cargo heat loss, in the event of a failure of the
heating system, is calculated to be only 1ºC.
The hull form of FS Charlotte has been designed to
minimise resistance to both friction and waves.
Resistance and powering tests were carried out in
Istanbul Technical University's Ata Nutku Ship Model
Testing Laboratory. Delta Marine claims that the overall
design provides an environment-friendly, cost-effective,
reliable, and flexible unit.
The tanker's classification covers an extensive list of
requirements from Bureau Veritas, including those for
vibration and noise, and the structure has additionally
been analysed to comply with the BV Veristar notation.
The propulsion plant consists of an MaK main
engine, having an output power of 5400kW at
500rev/min.
The transmission is taken through a
reduction gearbox (fitted with a power take-off shaft
for an alternator) to a CP propeller of 4400mm
diameter, turning at 150rev/min. Three 750kW heavy-
fuel-burning diesel generators are installed, and heat for
all cargo and domestic services is generated by two
thermal oil boilers and an economiser.
To increase manoeuvring capabilities, an 800kW bow
thruster is fitted, and there are four sets of two-drum
mooring winches, including two sets with windlasses.
Accommodation is arranged for 19 people, plus a
hospital and a pilot/owner's cabin, all with separate
toilet and shower.

HE Severnav shipyard is located at Drobeta Turnu
Severin, on the river Danube in western Romania,
near the Yugoslav border. Although better known as a
builder of specialist river/canal cargo vessels operating
on Russian and Eastern European waterways, this yard
has featured sea-going vessels, including tankers, in its
portfolio; nevertheless, Gaschem Shinano, considered to
be one of the most sophisticated ethylene tankers now
in service, gives the yard's operations an important new
thrust.
The design,
including gas plant layout and
engineering, was prepared by German consultant H B
Hunte Engineering in co-operation with the owner, and
features a single-deck, engine-aft hull with two cargo
holds. The aft one is single-skinned with upper wing
void spaces, and contains a bi-lobe cargo tank, whilst
the forward compartment is made box-shaped by a
double skin forming voids and ballast tanks, and has a
single cylindrical tank installed.
Both tanks are constructed of 5% nickel steel to a
design pressure of 5.5bar(g), and the plant is suitable
for transporting most semi-refrigerated cargoes in the
IMO Gas Code, within a temperature range of
-104
oC/+35
oC, and SG below 0.972. When carrying
chemicals, spool pieces are fitted in the piping system to
disconnect the reliquefaction plant, and these are also
used when two different cargoes are transported and
cooled at the same time.
The cargo tanks are not coated internally but are
insulated externally with a 170mm Cryopur foam
application. Deepwell pumps of 200m3/h duty are
installed in sumps at the aft end of the tanks to handle
cargo, with a 300m3/h booster pump positioned
amidships, near the manifold, to assist with long jetty
pipelines. Gas domes fitted to the front of the tanks
contain all pipe connections, with cargo operations
controlled by hydraulically actuated valves either locally
or from the cargo control office.
The reliquefaction plant is housed in an open shelter
on deck. This has a vapour area containing Burckhardt
(formerly Sulzer) compressors, suction separators and
economisers, and a liquid area fitted with multi-purpose
heat exchangers (MPHE) and modular refrigerant units;
the latter incorporate the cargo condensers and Mycon
double-screw compressors. The MPHE variously serve
as gas cooler, condenser, gas separator, gas heater (using
sea water as a heating medium) and vaporiser for
purging tanks before use. The complete gas-handling
plant was designed and supplied jointly by the owner
and HB Hunte Engineering.
A nitrogen plant is installed in the forecastle and
operates on the pressure-swing system, supplying
99.9% pure gas for padding and purging purposes, as
well as providing dry air of dew point -55
oC. The gas
plant is started locally but is remotely controlled by
computerised, redundant units with monitors and
alarms in important locations.
The main engine is of
MaK 9M32C design,
developing 4500kW at 600rev/min and driving a CP
propeller through a Renk gearbox for a service speed of
16knots at the 'ethylene' draught, and 17knots in ballast.
A 1500kW shaft-driven alternator is fitted, capable
alone of serving the needs of the reliquefaction plant,
and there are also two diesel-driven alternators of
1138kVA output available. Both main and auxiliary
engines are fresh-water-cooled using plate coolers, and
a thermal oil plant served from exhaust-gas and
auxiliary boilers maintains the bunker fuel temperature.
A Barke flap rudder and a 400kW bow thruster are
fitted to enhance manoeuvrability.

INGAPORE-based consultancy Shiptech Pte Ltd,
with a reference list of 16 newbuilding bulk cement
carriers, plus many conversions, in its portfolio, brings
more than 30 years' experience into the design of this
first newbuilding in the 14-strong Belden fleet. For the
first three years of its life, this vessel will be operating
on charter in the Caribbean/East Coast USA area.
The ship is laid out with a single deck, and poop and
forecastle erections. The engineroom, containing two
Wärtsilä 8L26A main engines, each developing 2640kW
and driving a CP propeller through a twin-input/single-
output gearbox, is positioned behind four pairs of
cargo holds equipped to handle two grades of ordinary
Portland cement of
specific density up to
1.2tonnes/m3. The general arrangement is completed
by a bulbous bow with cut-away forefoot, and an open-
water sternframe with bulb, allowing optimum wake
field, and an accommodation block above the
engineroom arranged with modular single cabins and
toilet blocks for 27 personnel.
Glory Atlantic is equipped with a Nordström cement
handling system, developed by MacGregor Bulk AB
(formerly BMH Marine), which allows mechanical
loading at 1000tonnes/hour using shore-chutes and a
single point receiving system each side of the vessel.
When loading pneumatically, an installed dust collector
allows loading rates up to 800tonnes/hour from two
lines. Discharge rates up to 1200tonnes/hour are
possible using a bulk discharge boom conveyor on deck,
and four sets of Nordström pneumatic blow-pump
systems installed in a pump room amidships, located
within a short double-hull configuration.
To discharge the cement, aeration panels sloped at
8deg and covered with a fabric material, are installed on
the tanktop throughout each hold, and by blowing air
through this material, the cement is fluidised and flows
towards the lowest point of the hold; here, it is collected
by vertical screw conveyors and transported to the deck
for discharge. Pneumatic unloading can be effected
using up to four pipelines, whilst mechanical unloading
is carried out through on-deck connections arranged to
transfer the cargo to tanker trucks ashore, or into
smaller vessels berthed alongside,
The gearbox is fitted with two power take-offs, each
driving 2200kW alternators which, when operating in
parallel, provide power for the cement discharge
processors. A 500kW diesel-alternator set is also
provided, and space is available in the funnel for future

receiving hopper, then on to the reversible screw
conveyor, from where the process continues as above.

fabric of sloped aeration panels installed on the tanktop

thence to shore. All surplus air circulating during these
operations is removed by means of deck-mounted dust

HE design of FPSO (floating, production, storage
and offloading) vessels has progressed through a
number of stages in their relatively short existence,

The owner of Searose originally favoured a positioning
system using azimuthing units both for propulsion and
maintaining location for its ship; however, Samsung,

tankers in the early 1990s, and with more conventional,
non-propelled FPSOs such as Bonga, built in 1992,
proposed what was claimed to be a more cost-effective

adopted for Nganhurra.

that quality was significantly improved when the ships

innovative system in 2003 (following earlier success
with tanker retrofits) for the newbuilding Aframax
tankers Tempera and Mastera (Significant Ships of 2003), in
which the fore end lines were designed for sailing in
open-water conditions, and the aft end lines and
structure were refined for running astern in thick ice.
The success of this interesting system owed much to
the coincident development of the ABB Azipod. This
made available a propulsion unit which could be turned
to drive the vessel either forward or astern, and, in the
latter mode, either 'drill through' the ice or contribute
towards reducing resistance by washing ice off the aft
body. The stern-running icebreaking capability of
Norilskiy Nickel is understood to be 1.5m of solid ice
plus 200mm of snow, at a minimum speed of 2knots.
In a change from the earlier tanker design, the new
vessel does have a heavily cut-away icebreaking stem,
whilst the stern features a duck-tail configuration with

leader in the field of ro-ro ships is emphasised by the

central skeg, claimed to offer reduced wave patterns, an
even wake field, and excellent water flow to the propellers.

ram-type steering gears.

feature a twisted leading edge, which gains maximum
benefit from the rotational energy produced behind the
propeller. Power take-offs from the two gearboxes each

EDERI Transatlantic was formed as recently as
2005 (but is also a respected name from the past),
from a merger of two Swedish companies, B&N
Nordsjöfrakt and Gorthon Lines. Each has a long
history of forest products transport, and it is as
replacements for units of the former Gorthon fleet
that Transpaper and two sisters have been built.
In service, the trio will operate on 15-year charters to
paper producer Stora Enso, running between Kemi and
Oulu in northern Finland and Gothenburg, Sweden,
which serves as a central hub for onward distribution to
UK and Belgium. The vessels have been specifically
designed to carry the company's special SECU (Stora
Enso Container Unit) containers: larger than a
conventional FEU, these can load up to 95tonnes each,
and 155 can be taken on board Transpaper, carried
mainly on special trailers.
Sto-ro traffic, trailers, road vehicles, cassettes,
conventional containers, and cars can also be carried,
using three fixed vehicle decks accessed over a stern
door/ramp to the main deck (No 3). Two fixed ramps,
complete with hoistable covers, are used to transfer
cargo down to deck 2, and up to deck 5, which is
partially covered forward by the superstructure.
All three decks are designed for loadings of
3tonnes/m2, and 24 SECUs can be accommodated on
deck 2, 62 on deck 3, and 69 on deck 5. Clear heights of
5.2m/5.2m and 7.2m (maximum), are available,
respectively, on these decks. A deep double bottom and
wide side spaces surround the cargo space below the
main deck, providing ballast tanks arranged to
counteract excessive heeling during cargo handling
operations, for which purpose an Intering anti-heeling
system is fitted. Also provided are stabilising tanks
positioned behind the superstructure on deck 7,
designed to reduce GM values during certain
conditions.
Unusually, since long shaft lines are generally 
frowned upon, Transpaper has been designed with the
engineroom well forward, under the superstructure,
with exhausts led away through a single funnel at the
starboard side. The propulsion installation comprises
twin MAN B&W 9L48/60B engines, each developing
9000kW at 514rev/min, and driving a single, stainless
steel, CP propeller at 130rev/min, through a Flender
twin-input/single-output Navilus gearbox.
The vessel can operate on either one or two engines
with respective service speeds of 15.10knots or
19.40knots. A power take-off from the gearbox
connects with an A van Kaick 3125kVA alternator, with
two Wärtsilä/A van Kaick diesel-driven alternator sets
of 1875kVA (1500kW) output also available.
Both main and auxiliary engines are fitted with SCR
(selective catalytic reduction) converters on their
exhaust lines. The aft body hull form incorporates a
central skeg and a transom stern in which a Becker
rudder is fitted, capable of turning 68.5deg P&S. Two
bow thrusters and a stern thruster, each of 800kW, are
fitted.
