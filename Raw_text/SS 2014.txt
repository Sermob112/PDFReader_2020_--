AMURA Shipbuilding Co. Ltd delivered 
Andalucian Zephyr, a 34,436dwt bulk 
carrier, to Three Kings Shipping Corp limited at 
its Imari Shipyard & Works on 12 March 2014. 
This vessel is the first vessel of the latest series of 
34,000dwt type bulk carriers called the High 
Bulk 34E, which has been developed in 
collaboration with one of the Namura group 
companies, the Hakodate Dock Co. UK, as a 
successor of the Hakodate Super Handy 32 
design with the specifications that have been 
drastically reviewed and modified from its 
predecessor to respond to the needs of today’s 
market.
The vessel is designed for the carriage of grain, 
coal, steel products, and logs/lumber as a bulk 
carrier, and the shallower draught hull form is 
designed to maximise the capacity. The vessel has 
five semi-box shaped cargo holds, without a bilge 
hopper, and each hold has a larger size hatch 
opening. In addition the vessel features collapsible/
folding and fixed type steel stanchions for log/
lumber loading on the upper deck.
Improved propulsion performance and fuel 
saving has been achieved with the adoption of the 
Namura flow Control Fin (NCF) and Rudder-fin 
(R-Fin), both developed by Namura, along with the 
optimisation of the hull form. Andalucian Zephyr is 
powered by a Mitsubishi 6UEC45LSE-B2 with a 
power output of 6,840kW at MCR and a service 
speed of 14.7knots.
The vessel has four sets of deck cranes that each 
have a 30tonne capacity and have been installed 
along the centreline in between the hatch covers for 
handling cargoes at ports that do not have cargo-
handling facilities. The water ballast tanks comply 
with the IMO PSPC-WBT regulations for corrosion 
protection to increase the safety of the vessel, and 
the main engine and generator engines conform to 
IMO NOx emission regulations (Tier II).

SIA Vision is the first in the series of six electric-
powered LNG carriers for US-based Chevron. The 
vessel was delivered in September from Samsung Heavy 
Industries (SHI) with sister vessel Asia Energy being 
delivered in September. A further two vessels will be 
delivered in 2015 with the last pair scheduled for 2016.
Asia Vision’s four centre cargo tanks have a total capacity 
of 160,000m
3 which uses Gaz Transport & Technigaz’s 
(GTT) Mark III membrane system and will keep the 
liquefied natural gas to a low temperature of -163°C, with 
a maximum daily boil-off rate of less than 0.15% of fully 
loaded cargo volume. The cargo system that has been 
installed  is designed to be capable of loading or 
discharging the LNG within 12 hours using eight pumps 
that have been supplied by EBRA International 
Corporation Cryodynamics Division and have a capacity 
of 1,850m
3/h.
Powering of Asia Vision is through the main generator 
engines, which consist of three sets of Wärtsilä 12V50DF 
and one Wärtsilä 6L50DF, which are installed in two 
dedicated spaces in the engine room. The generators 
have a power output of 11,700kW and 5,850kW, 
respectively and allow the vessel to have a service speed 
of 19.5knots.  Adding to this an energy saving device has 
been installed at the aft of the hull to enhance the vessel’s 
powering performance. 
The vessel is constructed in accordance with the rules 
and regulations of ABS classification society as an ocean 
going electric motor-driven LNG carrier. The design 
fatigue life is 40 years based on the ABS Rules SFA(40) 
procedure. A six tier deck house has also been provided for 
the accommodation of the crew.

ALT Flot 1 is the first in a series of a new design from 
the Marine Engineering Bureau (MEB), Russia of a 
wet and dry cargo vessel, the RST54 project that was 
delivered in October to Russian owner BF Tanker. The 
RST54 design has been developed to meet the 
requirements of expanding usage and development of 
the more narrow waterways in Russia, for which 
flexibility in the vessels operation has also been included 
in the design.
To be able to achieve this, the RST54 design has a 
maximum length and breadth based on its operational 
requirements and also has extremely fat hull contours. Balt 
Flot 1 is able to transport both dry and liquid cargoes, so 
the vessel can transport oil cargo when travelling to a 
destination and dry cargoes on the return journey. 
As with other river-sea going tankers designed by 
MEB, RST54 vessels use full-turn rudder propellers as a 
single movement and manoeuvre unit. The RST54 
design satisfies the Volga-Don canal dimensions such 
as the Volga-Balt way. Balt Flot 1's overall length is 
140.85m, breadth is 16.60m and depth 6.00m and 
comes under the Volga-Don max class.
Balt Flot 1 has 10 cargo tanks with a total capacity of 
5,446m
3, as well as two slop tanks. All cargo tanks are 
separate from the outer shell by double sides and double 
bottom and the dimensions of the double construction 
meet requirements of the International MARPOL 
73/78 convention.
The cargo system provides closed cargo 'receiving by 
shore' equipment and cargo discharge via the vessel’s 
submersible pumps. The manifolds provide cargo intake 
and discharge by both sides; they are arranged in the middle 
and aft parts of the cargo area. The transitional connectors 
have been used in order to provide manifolds coupling with 
different diameter hoses. The maximum loading capacity 
for each of the cargo or slop tanks is 600m
3/hour with 
loading of the whole vessel at 1,200m
3/hour.  
The cargo area on the upper deck 'with a 2m barrier' is 
intended for transportation of cargo that does not suffer 
from wetting (eg: gravel, rolled metal, heavy containers, cars, 
etc). The main deck shell within the cargo area is designed 
for loading 5.5t/m
2 and for grabs. The main loading 
condition is 4,700tonnes of gravel transportation on the 
open deck. A two-tier main deck has been included for the 
transportation of containers. In addition, the vessel can 
transport other containers reinforced to carry heavier 
weights such as 36tonne containers. 
The vessel also has the flexibility to extend its capacity and 
gain an additional two car decks, through the removal of 
certain structures.

ASCADE is the first in the series of the Emerald 
39,000dwt bulk carrier designs that was 
developed by Bestway Marine Engineering Design. 
The vessel was constructed by Jiangmen Nanyang 
Ship Engineering and delivered to its owner in June. 
Cascade is a typical double hull design with open 
hatch, which gives the vessel more flexibility in its 
cargo operation.
The design of the Emerald bulk carrier came from 
the need to develop eco-friendly vessels to meet 
with the needs of future environmental regulations. 
The company has said that economical and 
legislative drivers are motivating ship designers to 
consider the environmental impact of their designs. 
When initially starting out on the design Bestway 
looked at what was needed for an environmentally 
friendly ship, which consisted of the application of 
the latest and proven technologies, a vessel that 
would satisfy future rules and regulations and also 
the whole lifecycle of the vessel, including health, 
safety, environmental efficiency, efficiency through 
ship life, whilst still being financially viable for  
the owner.
The Emerald bulk carrier design features ship 
lines optimisation, which has been carried out using 
Napa and Friendship software. Also, the design has 
reduced resistance, along with a lightweight design. 
The structural optimisation of the vessel has looked 
at determining reasonable longitudinal strength 
allowance, increase in the percentage of high tensile 
steel usage and an improved arrangement and detail 
of structure.
Cascade has been fitted with a MAN B&W 
5S50ME-B9.2, which Bestway selected after 
calculation and analysis of some main engines, the 
5S50ME-B9 was the first choice for the main 
engine of the Emerald class of ships in terms of fuel 
oil consumption (FOC) and greenhouse gas 
emissions, as well as its high net present value 
(NPV). The FOC of the 5S50ME-B9.2 fitted on 
Cascade is 17.5tonnes/day at CSR with 15% sea 
margin when design loading.
In addition, further efficiencies were found from 
the propulsion system through the optimisation of 
the propeller, by looking at the blade design, the 
number of rotations and the diameter of the 
propeller, which all adds to the improvement of the 
flow field at the stern.
In order to further improve the efficiency of the 
vessel a large diameter and slower rotating 
propeller, which was designed by conventional 
MAU screw propeller diagrams has been fitted. 
Modern propeller design theory methods were 
adopted for the optimisation design, overall design 
qualities of the propeller such as the efficiency, 
cavitation, blade strength and screw weight are 
taken into account by the design package.
Further environmental features of Cascade are 
the reserved space onboard for a ballast water 
treatment system to be fitted at a later date. The 
hull of Cascade also has a hydrolysed type self-
polishing paint, which will decrease the vessel’s 
hull resistance.

pipelaying modes, along with heavy-lift capabilities

vessel’s hull design is based on that of a drillship 
allowing the vessel to operate in harsh conditions. The 
vessel is scheduled to be operational in the field in 
early 2015.

Crist shipyard, where the vessel was then outfitted by 
Lloyd Werft. The vessel was then fitted out at 
Huisman’s yard in Schiedam, The Netherlands, with

also constructed by Huisman, features an inclinable 
lay spread with the capacity to carry 9,000tonnes of 
pipe, Ceona Amazon, is also equipped to lay rigid 
and flexible pipelines and umbilicals, and to install 
Subsea structures using one or both of its 400tonne 
subsea cranes.

S-lay and rigid-lay construction equipment, which it 
calls its G-lay pipelaying system, it has been fitted to 
optimise deck space. Due to these features Ceona

pipe on and below deck. Instead of laying the pipe 
over a stern stinger, the pipe is guided forward over a 
deck aligner wheel, at the top of a dual-flexlay. The

straightener and two tensioners, with a combined

flexible installation reels and/or other structures.

pipe or 8,500tonnes of rigid pipe. The large deck area 
means that Ceona Amazon can carry out SURF and 
heavy-lift without the assistance of other vessels.

design. This allows Ceona Amazon to operate in 
rougher sea and wind conditions. The pipelaying

motions on the pipe installation stresses. The

HARLOTTE Schulte is the first vessel in a series of 
five 5,400TEU containerships that are being 
constructed at HHIC-Phil for German shipowner 
Bernhard Schulte. Charlotte Schulte was delivered in 
January 2014.
With the development of the Panama Canal, both owners 
and yards are looking at how to optimise vessels for the new 
dimensions of the canal. Because of this, vessels such as 
Charlotte Schulte, a containership with Post-Panamax 
dimensions, are starting to come on to the market. Charlotte 
Schulte has a 37.30m beam for the expected new expanded 
Panama Canal and has a 22m depth with a scantling 
draught of 13.90m, and weighs in at 65,000dwt.
These wide-beam box ships are starting to become more 
popular with owners. HHIC-Phil has explained the 
philosophy of this emerging vessel type has come about 
from the recession in the world shipping market and the 
rise in bunker prices, which caused shipping companies to 
have an interest in container vessels with optimum trims 
for slow-speed steaming that will reduce operational costs, 
rather than container vessels that are optimised for high-
speed steaming. 
To satisfy shipowners’ needs and IMO requirements, the 
lightweight and hull steel weight need to be minimised, 
which can be achieved by the wide beam design for 
container vessels. In addition, the benefit of the wide beam 
design enables more load capacity than other, same-class 
vessels and facilitates fuel cost reduction by adopting low-
speed engines.
In addition, owners are expecting substantial savings 
from this vessel type, which has been said to be around the 
US$17,000/day mark compared to older 5,500TEU 
Panamax designs.
The vessel has been fitted with a G-type super long 
stroke engine that meets with IMO Tier II NOx 
emission standards and the Phase III of IMO EEDI 
index. The engine has an output of 24,680kW SMCR at 
72rpm, to give a service speed of 21knots at NCR 
(22,212kW at 69.5rpm) with a 15% sea margin and 
design draught, which is designed to optimise the daily 
fuel consumption. The vessel runs on ultra-low sulphur 
marine gas oil (ULSMGO) and can be cooled by an 
ULSMGO cooler to increase the viscosity of the fuel to 
increase the performance of the main engine’s allowable 
operational range.
Charlotte Schulte is fitted with a fixed pitch five-bladed 
propeller that was designed and manufactured by HHIC 
and has been optimised to reduce the cavitation effects. Both 
the vessel’s hull and propeller optimisation were carried out 
by HHIC with DNV to give the vessel optimum 
performance parameters.

being maintained as China Shipping

Shipping Company (UASC) place orders for

Maersk McKinney Moller by another 1,000TEU.

Hyundai Heavy Industries (HHI) constructing the

between Europe and Asia.

carry some characteristics from the Triple E design,

has been arranged so that it is further forward on 
the vessel to enable better visibility and higher front 
deck loading. The original design was for an 
18,270TEU vessel but was then changed to 
19,000TEU as the increase in capacity required no 
further technical changes to the design.

empty containers for stability reasons. Onboard are

computer, which provides the data for the interface

with low load tuning by exhaust by-pass system for 
the turbocharger to achieve a better fuel 
consumption at practical operation condition.
A waste heat recovery system (WHR) system has 
also been installed for the main engine, and 
cylinder pressure, RPM and angle reference are 
calculated and presented on a ship’s computer in the 
engine control room. CSCL Globe is fitted with a 
full spade rudder with twisted leading edge that 
improves the propulsion efficiency by reducing 
rudder cavitation.
The vessel is arranged in a two island concept with 
the location of the accommodation separated from the 
engine room for crew comfort and also for optimum 
ballast tank arrangements to minimise the ballast 
amount at various loading conditions. In addition, 
CSCL Globe is fitted with two Hyundai Ecoballast 
water treatment systems that use both filter and UV 
technology and have a capacity of 1,500m

temperature and specific gravity are within the limits

EW tonnage in the ferry sector is much 
needed as the current fleet ages, but with new 
regulations coming in to play shipowners have 
been slow to react to this issue as it looms large on 
the horizon. However, Ignazio Messina’s fleet 
expansion has seen two series of eco-efficient 
multipurpose container ro-ro vessels ordered from 
two shipyards in Korea. The first vessel in the 
second series of four, Jolly Titanio, delivered from 
STX Offshore & Shipbuilding further builds on 
the efficiencies of the first series.
The main feature of the latest series of design is the 
flexibility in the vessels’ operation. The vessels in this 
series have not been designed for a specific route, but 
to allow the ships to cater for all worldwide routes, this 
includes new routes recently opened by the company, 
such as its North Europe routes. In order to meet with 
specific port requirements such as African ports, 
where an increase in deadweight as well as an increase 
in the beam has been selected and applied instead of 
an increase in draught.
The vessels have been designed for heavy cargoes 
and project cargoes, the ramp can bear loads of up to 
350tonnes and there is an area in the main garage able 
to accommodate a height of 7m. The in-take/out-take 
of the ship is served by a wide stern ramp (12m of 
clear way) and a wide stern door (27m of clear way), 
no elevators or movable ramps are applied, the design 
allows for operations to be carried out independently 
on the main garage, and above (main and upper 
decks) and below it (twin and hold deck). The 
maximum capacity of container loading is about 
2,238TEUs at ro-ro mode and about 2,889TEUs at 
shore mode. The ro-ro decks' capacity is about 6,065 
lane meters. Jolly Titanio is divided in to three decks, 
each deck is connected by a hydraulic ramp way. Two 
watertight division doors are located in the garage 
deck and one watertight division door is located in the 
tween deck.
Jolly Titanio is equipped with a controllable pitch 
propeller, which allows the vessel to be operated at 
different speeds and loading conditions, with the 
possibility to optimise the propulsion chain efficiency; 
a shaft generator in-line with the propeller shaft, 
frequency controlled, is able to supply all the electric 
power necessary during normal sailing and also 
during manoeuvring, reducing the necessity of diesel 
generators. Adding to the propulsion efficiency, Jolly 
Titanio is also fitted with a DSME pre-swirl stator, 
which is a set of fins placed in front of the propeller 
with the purpose of optimising the wake, with a mean 
power saving of around 4% observed at model tests. 
The MAN engine has also been fitted with a variable 
geometry turbo charger, able to improve the efficiency 
of the engine at low and intermediate loading, with an 
expected saving of around 4%.
To bring Jolly Titanio in line with forthcoming 
regulations it is equipped with a ballast water 
treatment system (UV-based), an alternative maritime 
power system (cold ironing) so the vessel is able to 
receive electric power from the shore when berthed in 
ports. The vessel is also equipped with an exhaust gas 
cleaning system, which Jolly Titanio is the first to be 
fitted with in the Messina fleet. The EGR will take the 
exhaust coming from the main engine, diesel 
generators and auxiliary boiler and pass it through the 
scrubber, with a hybrid system able to operate both in 
closed and open loops.

HE shipping industry has been under pressure 
to reduce its carbon emissions, in response to 
this pressure Seaspan has designed and developed 
the SAVER 10000 series, which balances cost 
effectiveness and client acceptability with energy 
efficiency measures. The vessel, MOL Bravo, is the 
first in the second series constructed at Jiangsu 
Yangzi Zinfu Shipbuilding and delivered to 
Seaspan in July.
The SAVER 10000 breaks with the traditional 
method of optimising for a design point of one speed 
and draught that is very seldom seen during the ship’s 
operation. The SAVER 10000 is instead optimised to 
operate over a range of speeds and draughts, based on 
Seaspan’s extensive experience with this vessel type. 
Seaspan has suggested this is a paradigm shift in the 
container industry rather than an iterative 
improvement, as prior to the design of the SAVER 
10000 this approach of optimising for the anticipated 
operational profile was uncommon.
Through numerous model tests, the hull lines and 
bulbous bow have been optimised and matched to the 
engine and propeller to ensure maximum energy 
efficiency over the range of speeds and draughts the vessel 
is likely to see in service. The efficiency of the vessel has 
been further improved with the installation of a full spade 
rudder with twisted leading edge, Propeller Boss cap fins 
and the use of high-performance low friction anti-fouling 
paint. Installation of a trim and draught optimisation 
module as part of the loading software, will allow the vessel 
to gain further efficiencies on each voyage. Later sister 
vessels in this series will also have Becker Twisted Fins 
which will decrease the fuel consumption even further.
The main engine is a MAN B&W 10S90ME-C9, an 
efficient super long-stroke slow-speed engine. The engine 
is derated and has exhaust gas bypass with low load tuning, 
allowing greater fuel savings at lower loads where the 
vessels are more often operating. The addition of PMI auto 
tuning will ensure the engine is always optimised. The load 
on the auxiliary engines has been reduced by the 
installation of frequency controlled sea water pumps and 
engine room fans.
The SAVER 10000 has also been designed to reduce 
other environmental impacts; the vessel has a Tier II 
engine to reduce its NOx emissions, it has an RWO 
CleanBallast ballast water treatment system. An Inventory 
of Hazardous Materials certificate will assist in the safe and 
environmental recycling of the vessel at the end of its life 
and it has DNV Clean Notation to validate the vessel’s 
design and equipment are in compliance with or are more 
extensive than those found in international standards 
currently in force.

range Phoenix was the first bulk carrier to adopt 
the NSafe-Hull design. The vessel was launched 
for Mitsui O.S.K Lines at the Saijo Shipyard of 
Imabari Shipbuilding in August. Although not the 
first vessel in the order, this is the first vessel that has 
had this concept applied to its design.
The NSafe-Hull (a highly ductile steel plate for 
shipbuilding with improved collision safety) was 
developed by Nippon Steel & Sumitomo Metal 
Corporation (NSSMC), and the material was first 
applied to the bulker through collaborative R&D by 
NSSMS, Imabari Shipbuilding and the National 
Maritime Research Institute (NMRI).
Imabari Shipbuilding highlights that the NSafe-
Hull has excellent ductility, while maintaining the 
workability (eg: machinability and weldability) of 
conventional steels, which substantially improves the 
ship’s collision safety.  NSSMC developed NSafe-Hull 
through designing chemical composition and 
microstructural control in crystalline scale.
If a ship is made with an NSafe-Hull the energy 
absorbed before the breaking of the hull could be 
roughly triple that of a ship constructed of 
conventional steels, in the case of a side collision. It 
can, therefore, be said that the NSafe-Hull increases 
ship durability compared to those constructed with 
conventional steels. The NSafe-Hull used around 
3,000tonnes in total of the new metal which was 
included only in the structural parts where high 
collision safety performance was required, such as 
the side plates of cargo holds and fuel tanks. 
The 206,600dwt Orange Phoenix measures 299.94m 
in length overall with a breadth of 50.00m and depth 
of 24.70m. The vessel is powered by a Hitachi Zosen 
Corporation 6S70ME-C8.2 that has a power output 
of 16,420kW and gives the vessel a service speed of 
14.6knots.

IETER Schelte is the latest and largest platform 
installation/removal and pipelaying vessel to be 
constructed to date for Belgium owner Allseas and was 
delivered to the owner in October.
Pieter Schelte, named after the offshore heavy lifting 
pioneer Pieter Schelte Heerema (1908 - 1981), is a vessel 
for the single-lift installation and removal of large 
offshore oil and gas platforms and the installation of oil 
and gas pipelines. Designed in-house by Allseas as with 
its predecessors, also named Pieter Schelte, represents 
the epitome of Allseas’ engineering and innovation the 
owner highlights.
Due to its large lifting capacity the latest Pieter Schelte 
will drastically reduce the amount of offshore work 
associated 
with 
platform 
installation 
or 
decommissioning, largely moving this work to shore. 
Executing topside removal in a single lift will 
significantly reduce the man-hours spent on offshore 
cleaning, partitioning, installing lifting points and 
rigging platform modules. This, in turn, considerably 
reduces health and safety risks. It will also reduce the 
environmental risks. 
Separating modules offshore requires a large amount 
of piping to be cut, which carries a risk of oil and 
chemical residue leakage; such risks are removed with 
single-lift removal.   
The pipelaying equipment makes it possible to install 
record-weight pipelines from shallow to ultra-deep water, 
and achieve high lay rates. The tensioner capacity of 
2,000tonnes, doubling the capacity of the world’s largest 
pipelaying vessel, Allseas’ Solitaire, will make Pieter Schelte 
a notable vessel in the pipelaying arena. 
The ability to sail under its own power at a high transit 
speed makes the vessel suitable for worldwide use. 
Pieter Schelte’s large ship size gives it very good wave 
response behaviour, superior to semi-submersible crane 
vessels in operational wave conditions; topsides and 
jackets can be installed or removed in significant wave 
heights of up to 3.5m.
One of the projects that Pieter Schelte will work on is the 
removal of the Shell Brent Delta platform, which is planned 
later in the summer of 2015, or in the spring of 2016.

IRGOS is the latest development of a 9,000dwt 
environmentally friendly general cargo vessel that has 
been designed by Turkish naval architects, Norden. The 
vessel has been constructed at Anrun Ship, China and was 
delivered to its owner Elefteros Maritime in May, with a 
further two vessels still on order. The original design 
capacity was 7,600dwt, and Norden designed it to meet 
more stringent damage stability requirements and classed 
the vessel as “Type B-60”. This has allowed the design to 
have a 60% reduced freeboard, increasing the capacity.
Adding to the design’s green credentials is an optimised 
hull form and various green technology solutions to meet 
with the RINa class requirements for its Green+ notation 
and Green Passport, both of which were brought into effect 
by the Italian Class society to allow shipowners to develop 
more efficient ships and to be able to accredit the notation to 
the vessel. Having the Green+ notation also means that the 
vessel complies with the IMO ship recycling 
recommendations.
The hull form, which has a more modern bow, was 
created in-house and optimised through computational 
fluid dynamics (CFD) to give the vessel better seakeeping 
characteristics. This has been combined with a high 
efficiency propeller and boss cap fins, which breaks the hub 
vortex, which allows the vessel to achieve a fully loaded 
speed of 15.5knots with a fuel consumption of 8tonnes/day 
of HFO. This alone has reduced the fuel consumption by 7% 
compared to more traditional propeller setups.
The vessel is fitted with an MHI 6UEC33LS that has a 
power output of 2,260kW. The vessel’s operational speed of 
10knots will further reduce its fuel consumption to 
5.5tonnes/day. The vessel requires 0.4tonnes/day of MDO at 
EU port idle condition and by utilising the solar power and 
cold ironing facilities can achieve zero emissions in port, it 
has been said.
Apart from hull and main machinery, there are more 
systems that affect the consumption figures both directly 
and indirectly. As part of low emission goal and creating a 
green vessel, further technologies such as 20kW solar panels 
have been installed on the wheelhouse with a direct feed into 
the electrical system.

ITSUBISHI Heavy Industries’ (MHI) latest 
LNG carrier design, named Sayaendo 
(meaning peas in a pod), has been developed as an 
environmentally friendly vessel that reduces 
operating and maintenance costs. The first of the 
design to be constructed, Seishu Maru, was delivered 
to its owner Trans Pacific Shipping 1 in September, 
with a further seven vessels of the design to follow.
The 155,696m
3 capacity LNG carrier has a 
continuous cover which houses the four spherical 
tanks, developed by MHI and Aker Arctic. The cover 
acts as a hull-reinforcement element, resulting in 
greater overall strength and reduction in weight. In 
addition the Sayaendo design is capable of 
transporting 8,000m
3 more LNG than a typical 
147,000m
3 capacity carrier, without the need to 
increase the beam. This has been achieved by using 
vertically stretched spherical tanks that maintain the 
same tank diameter. Through this the latest design 
provides a higher cargo capacity while meeting the 
latest requirements of the Panama Canal.
To achieve a more environmentally friendly profile 
measures have been taken to reduce fuel 
consumption. Further optimisation of the design has 
seen a reduction of 5-10% in the ship’s weight, 
propulsion performance along with reduction in the 
longitudinal wind force with the use of the 
continuous tank cover and the adoption of the high-
efficiency ultra-steam turbine (UST) propulsion 
plant. With its fuel-selection flexibility the UST 
provides cost-efficient choices in managing the 
effects of full price fluctuations.
MHI says that it expects that the Sayaendo design 
will be able to achieve a CO2 reduction of 
approximately 25% per cargo unit during actual 
operations compared with conventional 147,000m
3 
LNG carriers.
By altering the thickness of the thermal insulator 
thanks to the characteristics of the spherical tank 
system, the rates of naturally generated boil off gas 
(BOG), which is caused by heat ingress into LNG 
storage tanks, can be readily optimised according to 
operational requirements. Instead of a typical BOR 
of 0.15%/d achieved in conventional LNG carriers, 
the Sayaendo design is capable of 0.080%/d, which is 
even lower than the 0.10%/d adopted recently as the 
low boil-off rate, MHI claims.
Added features of the design include its 
independent tank system, allowing the vessel to be 
tolerant to local ice impact. Overall hull girder 
strengthening also means that the vessel is highly 
effective in resisting global ice impact loads.

itsubishi Heavy Industries (MHI) 
completed construction of the cargo-
passenger ship Tachibana-Maru and delivered 
the vessel to Tokai Kisen at its Shimonoseki 
Shipyard and Machinery Works on 4 June 2014. 
The vessel is a “super eco-ship” with a high-
efficiency propulsion system that achieves 
outstanding energy savings, environmental load 
reduction, and enhanced passenger comfort and 
safety. The ship began operating the service on 
the route between Tokyo and the Izu Islands from 
27 June 2014.
The ship employs a tandem-hybrid contra-rotating 
propeller (CRP) propulsion system developed by 
MHI. The system is comprised of single-shaft 
propulsion propellers driven by low-speed marine 
diesel engine and electric-drive azimuth propulsion 
propellers that enable the direction of propulsion 
power to be freely changed. In comparison to 
conventional twin-shaft propulsion systems, the new 
system enables less appendages for propulsion 
systems such as skegs, shaft brackets and rudders and 
reduces water resistance, and improves energy-
saving efficiency by allowing the use of only fuel-
efficient low-speed diesel power in bays and channels 
where slower navigation speeds are required.
The latest propulsion system enables Tachibana-
Maru to achieve 15.6% higher fuel efficiency than 
Salvia-Maru, a cargo-passenger ship built by MHI 
and currently in service at Tokai Kisen. In addition, 
the use of an electronically controlled fuel injection 
system enables the main low-speed marine diesel 
engine to achieve higher combustion efficiency, 
thereby ensuring that Tachibana-Maru emits less 
carbon dioxide (CO2) and nitrogen oxides (NOx). 
The ship is also equipped with a system that utilises 
exhaust gas thermal energy more efficiently.
The propulsion system's ability to deliver the 
required power using only a single-shaft configuration, 
with the load shared between a single diesel engine 
and an electric drive system, ensures higher passenger 
comfort by reducing both noise and vibration. In 
addition, the electric-drive azimuth propulsion system 
can be used as a stern thruster in combination with 
the bow thruster to enhance safety and swiftness when 
approaching or departing the pier.

ensure the integrity of the cargo tanks and to keep to the

T has been said that the ferry segment is over due an 
update in new tonnage, but with a financial downturn 
and environmental regulations coming in to effect this 
has not been an easy action for shipowners to carry out. 
However, Nordana is one shipowner that has stepped 
forward to renew its fleet and to incorporate some of the 
latest technologies on the market.
Nordana’s latest eco ro-ro, Wedellsborg, was originally 
designed and ordered as a one off, but with an option to 
construct more. Wedellsborg was constructed at Canteri 
Navali Visentini, Italy and delivered to the owner in June. 
Since the launch of Wedellsborg Nordana has announced 
that it would also be introducing Williamsborg (previously 
Beachy Head), which joined the service in July, 
commencing service in Italy. A third vessel, Wilhelmsborg 
is scheduled to join the service in the first quarter of 2015, 
whereafter the tonnage renewal will be complete. The 
three-vessel service will overall increase the capacity on 
this route by about 30%, and enable Nordana to maintain 
a fixed-day three weekly schedule the company noted.
Wedellsborg was designed by NAOS Ship and Boat 
Design in Italy. The vessel is a standard ro-ro design, but 
features more optimised cargo space and cargo lane layout. 
In addition, the stern ramp has been widened, which 
allows simultaneous loading of vehicles. Wedellsborg also 
has an increased capacity of about 35% compared to 
Nordana’s present tonnage.
The cargo lanes have been improved by opening up 
more space through the reduction of casing and engine 
space above the main deck. These areas have been 
designed to allow the usage of all spaces available below the 
fixed ramp, which has also been reduced from 9m to 4.5m 
in width; the elimination of the main vertical trunk 
containing the ventilators for the lower hold, allows the 
vessel to have continuous straight lanes to the extreme fore 
end at each cargo level.
The vessel's design and energy efficient engine have also 
added to the vessel’s overall efficiency, which is expected to 
have a fuel consumption of 30tonnes/24hrs at design speed. 
To achieve this, NAOS carried out extensive CFD studies 
(based on the FutureShip code) on many different hull 
shapes. Wedellsborg has a free-flow art form and a revised 
FlexBow and has a low Froude number, with tank and 
cavitation tests being carried out by Brodarski Institute, 
Zagreb. The FlexBow of this vessel has also been optimised 
for its service speed of 19knots.
Wedellsborg has four vehicle decks with a total lane 
length of 2,546m and has a capacity of 50 cars on the main 
deck and 50 cars on the upper deck. The vessel also has a 
total TEU capacity of 773 and is accessed through the stern 
of the ship.
